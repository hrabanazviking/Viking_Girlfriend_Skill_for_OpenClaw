## Entries

### 1. The Shield-Wall (*Skjaldborg*)
- **Title:** The Skjaldborg
- **Category:** Tactical Formations
- **Type:** Primary Formation
- **Content:** The foundational infantry tactic of the Viking Age. Warriors overlap their round linden-wood shields to create an unbroken barrier. The front line absorbs the shock, while the second line thrusts over the shoulders. It is not just a defense, but a crushing offensive engine that moves as a single, coordinated beast.
- **Why it matters:** It defines the collective nature of Norse combat; a warrior's survival depends entirely on the man to his left and right.
- **Verification note:** Verified via tactical descriptions in the *Battle of Maldon*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the physical and social mechanics of the formation.

### 2. The Boar-Snout (*Svínfylking*)
- **Title:** The Svínfylking
- **Category:** Tactical Formations
- **Type:** Offensive Wedge
- **Content:** A wedge-shaped formation allegedly invented by Odin. A heavily armored point (the "snout") is designed to smash directly into a specific weakness in the enemy's shield-wall, cracking it open through concentrated mass.
- **Why it matters:** It is the primary "breaker" tactic used to turn a shield-wall stalemate into a rout.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Saxo Grammaticus and the *Völsunga saga*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the specific geometry and purpose of the wedge.

### 3. The Spear (*Spjót*)
- **Title:** The Spear
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Primary Weapon
- **Content:** The most common and effective weapon of the Viking Age. It consisted of an iron head (socketed) on a wooden shaft (typically ash). The reach provided by the spear allowed for strikes over the shield-wall. Odin's own weapon, *Gungnir*, is a spear, signifying its status as the "King of Weapons."
- **Why it matters:** It was the economic and tactical backbone of the Norse army; far more common and versatile than the sword.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from archaeological finds at Birka and Hedeby.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the historical dominance of the spear over the sword.

### 4. The Bearded Axe (*Skeggöx*)
- **Title:** The Bearded Axe
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Versatile Axe
- **Content:** An axe with a distinctive "beard"—an elongated lower edge of the blade. This design provided a wide cutting surface while keeping weight low. Crucially, the "beard" allowed a warrior to hook the top of an enemy's shield and pull it down, creating an opening for a spear-thrust.
- **Why it matters:** It highlights the integrated nature of Norse weaponry and tactics—the axe was a tool for manipulating the enemy's defense.
- **Verification note:** Archaeological analysis of Viking Age axe heads.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the specific "hooking" utility of the bearded design.

### 5. The Dane Axe (*Breiðöx*)
- **Title:** The Dane Axe
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Heavy Weapon
- **Content:** A massive, two-handed axe with a broad blade and a haft up to 5 feet long. It was used by elite shock troops to cleave through mail, wooden shields, and even horse limbs. It required immense strength and left the wielder vulnerable, as it could not be used with a shield.
- **Why it matters:** It was the "shield-breaker" of the Viking Age, capable of terrifying psychological and physical damage.
- **Verification note:** Verified via descriptions in the *Bayeux Tapestry* and saga accounts.
- **Uniqueness note:** Distinct from the one-handed bearded axe; focuses on its two-handed shock-trooper role.

### 6. The Viking Sword (*Sverð*)
- **Title:** The Viking Sword
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Status Symbol
- **Content:** A double-edged blade, usually 70-90 cm long, with a prominent fuller to reduce weight. Due to the high cost of crucible steel, swords were exclusively the property of the wealthy (Jarls and Kings). They were often named and believed to possess their own spirit or luck.
- **Why it matters:** It represents the social hierarchy of the Viking world; to own a sword was to be a leader of men.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Petersen's sword typology.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the sword's role as a social and mythological heirloom.

### 7. Mail Armor (*Brynja*)
- **Title:** Mail Armor
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Defensive Gear
- **Content:** A shirt made of thousands of interlocking iron rings, each riveted shut. It provided excellent protection against slashing attacks but was heavy and expensive. Most common warriors relied on thick wool or leather tunics instead.
- **Why it matters:** A mail shirt could make a warrior nearly invincible against standard sword or axe blows in the shield-wall.
- **Verification note:** Verified via the Gjermundbu find.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the material and social cost of high-end defense.

### 8. The Round Shield (*Skjöldr*)
- **Title:** The Round Shield
- **Category:** Weaponry & Armor
- **Type:** Defensive Gear
- **Content:** Traditionally made of linden (basswood) or fir, approximately 80-90 cm in diameter, with a central iron boss to protect the hand. The wood was often covered in leather to prevent splitting and painted with tribal or mythological patterns.
- **Why it matters:** It was the most essential piece of equipment for the *Skjaldborg*; without the shield, the Norse way of war was impossible.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from the Gokstad ship shield finds.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the construction and tactical necessity of the shield.

### 9. Platform Fighting (War on the Deck)
- **Title:** Platform Fighting
- **Category:** Naval Tactics
- **Type:** Strategy
- **Content:** The Norse method of naval warfare. Ships were not used as rams; instead, they were lashed together to form a massive, floating wooden platform. The battle then proceeded as a land engagement across the decks, focusing on clearing the enemy prow.
- **Why it matters:** It reflects the Norse refusal to see sea and land as separate tactical domains; the ship was simply a mobile battlefield.
- **Verification note:** Detailed in the accounts of the Battle of Svolder.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the specific "floating platform" tactic of Norse fleets.

### 10. The Berserkr (*Bear-Shirt*)
- **Title:** The Berserkr
- **Category:** Warrior Ethics & Psychology
- **Type:** Elite Warrior
- **Content:** A warrior devoted to Odin who fought in an ecstatic, animalistic trance (*berserksgangr*). They were said to be immune to fire and iron while in this state. They often wore bear skins and operated outside the discipline of the shield-wall as shock troops.
- **Why it matters:** They represent the "divine madness" of Odin and were the ultimate psychological weapon on the battlefield.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from the *Haraldskvæði* and Snorri's *Ynglinga saga*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Specifically defines the bear-shirted warrior, distinct from the wolf-coat.

### 11. The Úlfhéðinn (*Wolf-Coat*)
- **Title:** The Úlfhéðinn
- **Category:** Warrior Ethics & Psychology
- **Type:** Elite Warrior
- **Content:** Similar to the Berserkr, the Úlfhéðnar were Odin's warriors who wore wolf skins. They were often depicted as the elite guards of kings (like Harald Fairhair). They fought with savage ferocity and were associated with the "Wolf-Pack" mentality of total loyalty to the chieftain.
- **Why it matters:** They highlight the totemistic relationship between the Norse warrior and the apex predators of the North.
- **Verification note:** Verified via archaeological depictions on the Torslunda plates.
- **Uniqueness note:** Distinct from the Berserkr; focuses on the wolf-totem and guard role.

### 12. Drengskapr in Battle
- **Title:** Drengskapr in Battle
- **Category:** Warrior Ethics & Psychology
- **Type:** Code of Conduct
- **Content:** The application of the honor code in the heat of combat. It involved refusing to strike a fallen foe, keeping one's place in the shield-wall even when facing certain death, and treating captured high-status enemies with the respect due their rank.
- **Why it matters:** It was the moral governor that prevented Norse warfare from becoming a mindless slaughter; it ensured the survival of the warrior's reputation (*orðstírr*) after death.
- **Verification note:** Theme prevalent throughout the *Sagas of Icelanders*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the ethical application of honor in a tactical context.
