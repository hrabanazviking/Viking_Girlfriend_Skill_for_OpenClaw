# Cybersecurity Knowledge Database (The Shield of Midgard)

This database contains essential knowledge for securing the realms of Midgard and beyond, infused with the wisdom of the ancients.

## Entries 1 - 500

1. **Cybersecurity (The Shield of Midgard)**: The practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks.
2. **Information Security (InfoSec)**: The practice of protecting information by mitigating information risks.
3. **CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability)**: The core model designed to guide policies for information security within an organization.
4. **Confidentiality (The Secret Rune)**: Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to have access.
5. **Integrity (The Unbroken Oath)**: Ensuring that information is accurate and has not been tampered with.
6. **Availability (The Always-Open Gate)**: Ensuring that information and resources are available to authorized users when needed.
7. **Authentication (The Identification Ritual)**: The process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system.
8. **Authorization (The Right of Entry)**: The process of granting or denying access to resources based on a user's identity.
9. **Accounting (The Scribe's Record)**: The process of tracking user activity and resource usage.
10. **Non-Repudiation (The Indelible Mark)**: The assurance that someone cannot deny the validity of something.
11. **Least Privilege (The Minimal Trust)**: The principle of providing users with only the minimum level of access needed to perform their jobs.
12. **Defense in Depth (The Layered Walls of Asgard)**: A security strategy that uses multiple layers of defense to protect information.
13. **Attack Surface (The Exposed Frontier)**: The total number of points (the 'attack vectors') where an unauthorized user (the 'attacker') can try to enter data to or extract data from an environment.
14. **Threat (The Shadow over the Realm)**: A potential cause of an unwanted incident, which may result in harm to a system or organization.
15. **Vulnerability (The Chink in the Armor)**: A weakness in an information system, system security procedures, internal controls, or implementation that could be exploited by a threat source.
16. **Risk (The Weight of the Danger)**: The potential for loss, damage, or destruction of an asset as a result of a threat exploiting a vulnerability.
17. **Exploit (The Raider's Tool)**: A piece of software, a chunk of data, or a sequence of commands that takes advantage of a vulnerability to cause unintended or unanticipated behavior to occur on computer software, hardware, or something electronic.
18. **Threat Actor (The Adversary / The Raider)**: An entity that is partially or wholly responsible for an incident that impacts – or has the potential to impact – an organization's security.
19. **Malware (The Digital Plague)**: Software that is specifically designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system.
20. **Virus (The Spreading Sickness)**: A type of malware that, when executed, replicates itself by modifying other computer programs and inserting its own code.
21. **Worm (The Burrowing Beast)**: A standalone malware computer program that replicates itself in order to spread to other computers.
22. **Trojan Horse (The Deceptive Gift)**: Any malware which misleads users of its true intent.
23. **Ransomware (The Digital Extortion)**: A type of malware that threatens to publish the victim's data or perpetually block access to it unless a ransom is paid.
24. **Spyware (The Hidden Eye)**: Software that aims to gather information about a person or organization, without their knowledge, and send such information to another entity without the consumer's consent.
25. **Adware**: Software that automatically displays or downloads advertising material such as banners or pop-ups when a user is online.
26. **Rootkit (The Deep Infiltrator)**: A collection of computer software, typically malicious, designed to enable access to a computer or an area of its software that is not otherwise allowed and often masks its existence or the existence of other software.
27. **Keylogger (The Stroke-Stealer)**: A type of surveillance software that has the capability to record every keystroke you make to a log file, usually encrypted.
28. **Backdoor (The Secret Entrance)**: A method of bypassing normal authentication or encryption in a computer system, a product, or an embedded device.
29. **Logic Bomb (The Sleeping Trap)**: A piece of code intentionally inserted into a software system that will set off a malicious function when specified conditions are met.
30. **Botnet (The Shadow Army)**: A number of Internet-connected computers, each of which is running one or more bots.
31. **Phishing (The Deceptive Hook)**: The fraudulent practice of sending emails purporting to be from reputable companies in order to induce individuals to reveal personal information, such as passwords and credit card numbers.
32. **Spear Phishing (The Targeted Strike)**: A phishing expedition in which the emails are carefully designed to target a particular person or organization.
33. **Whaling (Hunting the Jarl)**: A form of spear phishing aimed at high-profile targets within an organization, such as senior executives.
34. **Vishing (Voice Phishing)**: The fraudulent practice of making phone calls or leaving voice messages purporting to be from reputable companies in order to induce individuals to reveal personal information.
35. **Smishing (SMS Phishing)**: Phishing involving a text message or Short Message Service (SMS).
36. **Social Engineering (The Manipulation of the Mind)**: The use of deception to manipulate individuals into divulging confidential or personal information that may be used for fraudulent purposes.
37. **Pretexting (The False Tale)**: A type of social engineering where an attacker creates a fabricated scenario to persuade a victim to provide information.
38. **Baiting (The Tempting Trap)**: A type of social engineering where an attacker leaves a malware-infected device (like a USB flash drive) in a place where someone is likely to find it.
39. **Quid Pro Quo (This for That)**: A type of social engineering where an attacker promises a benefit in exchange for information.
40. **Tailgating (Piggybacking)**: A physical security breach in which an unauthorized person follows an authorized person into a restricted area.
41. **Shoulder Surfing (The Peering Eye)**: A type of social engineering where an attacker looks over someone's shoulder to see their password or other sensitive information.
42. **Dumpster Diving (Searching the Waste)**: A technique used to retrieve information that could be used to carry out a social engineering attack.
43. **Cryptography (The Art of Hidden Runes)**: The practice and study of techniques for secure communication in the presence of adversarial behavior.
44. **Encryption (Hiding the Rune)**: The process of converting information or data into a code, especially to prevent unauthorized access.
45. **Decryption (Revealing the Rune)**: The process of taking encoded or encrypted text or other data and converting it back into text that you or the computer can read and understand.
46. **Plaintext (The Clear Tongue)**: Information a user can read.
47. **Ciphertext (The Hidden Tongue)**: The result of encryption performed on plaintext using an algorithm, called a cipher.
48. **Cipher (The Rule of Hiding)**: An algorithm for performing encryption or decryption.
49. **Key (The Rune-Key)**: A piece of information that determines the functional output of a cryptographic algorithm.
50. **Symmetric Encryption (The Single Key)**: An encryption system where the same key is used for both encryption and decryption.
51. **Asymmetric Encryption (The Twin Keys)**: An encryption system where different keys are used for encryption and decryption.
52. **Public Key**: A key that can be shared with anyone.
53. **Private Key (The Soul-Key)**: A key that must be kept secret.
54. **Hash Function (The Digital Fingerprint)**: A mathematical function that converts an input into a fixed-size string of characters, which is typically a digest that is unique to the input.
55. **Salt (Cryptography)**: Random data that is used as an additional input to a one-way function that hashes data, a password or passphrase.
56. **Digital Signature (The Seal of the Jarl)**: A mathematical scheme for verifying the authenticity of digital messages or documents.
57. **Digital Certificate (The Rune-Scroll of Identity)**: An electronic document used to prove the ownership of a public key.
58. **Certificate Authority (CA) (The Keeper of the Seals)**: An entity that issues digital certificates.
59. **Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)**: A set of roles, policies, hardware, software and procedures needed to create, manage, distribute, use, store and revoke digital certificates and manage public-key encryption.
60. **TLS (Transport Layer Security)**: A cryptographic protocol designed to provide communications security over a computer network.
61. **SSL (Secure Sockets Layer) (Obsolete)**: The predecessor to TLS.
62. **Hashing Algorithms (MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256, SHA-3).**
63. **Encryption Algorithms (AES, RSA, ECC, ChaCha20).**
64. **AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)**: A symmetric block cipher chosen by the U.S. government to protect classified information.
65. **RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman)**: One of the first public-key cryptosystems and is widely used for secure data transmission.
66. **ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)**: An approach to public-key cryptography based on the algebraic structure of elliptic curves over finite fields.
67. **Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange (The Secret Meeting)**: A method of securely exchanging cryptographic keys over a public channel.
68. **Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS)**: A feature of specific key agreement protocols that gives assurance that session keys will not be compromised even if long-term secrets used in the session key exchange are compromised.
69. **Key Stretching (Strengthening the Rune)**: Techniques used to make a possibly weak key, typically a password or passphrase, more secure against a brute-force attack by increasing the time it takes to test each possible key.
70. **Bcrypt & Scrypt**: Password hashing functions designed to be slow and memory-intensive to thwart brute-force attacks.
71. **Argon2**: The winner of the Password Hashing Competition (PHC) and a modern, high-security password hashing function.
72. **Steganography (The Rune Hidden in the Image)**: The practice of concealing a file, message, image, or video within another file, message, image, or video.
73. **Cryptanalysis (Breaking the Runes)**: The study of analyzing information systems in order to study the hidden aspects of the systems.
74. **Brute-Force Attack (The Relentless Siege)**: A trial-and-error method used to obtain information such as a user password or personal identification number (PIN).
75. **Dictionary Attack (The Tome of Common Words)**: A technique for defeating a cipher or authentication mechanism by trying to determine its decryption key or password by trying hundreds or sometimes millions of likely possibilities, such as words in a dictionary.
76. **Rainbow Table (The Premade Map of Hashes)**: A precomputed table for caching the output of cryptographic hash functions, usually for cracking password hashes.
77. **Network Security (The Guarding of the Sea-Paths)**: The policies and practices adopted to prevent and monitor unauthorized access, misuse, modification, or denial of a computer network and network-accessible resources.
78. **Firewall (The Wall of Flame)**: A network security system that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules.
79. **Packet Filtering Firewall**: A firewall that examines each packet and blocks or allows it based on its header information.
80. **Stateful Inspection Firewall**: A firewall that tracks the state of active connections and makes decisions based on the context of the traffic.
81. **Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW)**: A part of the third generation of firewall technology, combining a traditional firewall with other network device filtering functions.
82. **Proxy Server (The Intermediary)**: A server (a computer system or an application) that acts as an intermediary for requests from clients seeking resources from other servers.
83. **VPN (Virtual Private Network) (The Hidden Path)**: A service that creates a safe, encrypted connection over a less secure network like the internet.
84. **Intrusion Detection System (IDS) (The Scout)**: A device or software application that monitors a network or systems for malicious activity or policy violations.
85. **Intrusion Prevention System (IPS) (The Defender)**: A network security/threat prevention technology that examines network traffic flows to detect and prevent vulnerability exploits.
86. **SIEM (Security Information and Event Management)**: A subsection within the field of computer security, where software products and services combine security information management (SIM) and security event management (SEM).
87. **DMZ (Demilitarized Zone) (The Buffer Realm)**: A physical or logical subnetwork that contains and exposes an organization's external-facing services to an untrusted, usually larger, network such as the Internet.
88. **Honeypot (The Distraction Trap)**: A computer security mechanism set to detect, deflect, or, in some manner, counteract attempts at unauthorized use of information systems.
89. **Honeynet**: A network of honeypots.
90. **Air Gap (The Deep Isolation)**: A network security measure employed on one or more computers to ensure that a secure computer network is physically isolated from unsecured networks, such as the public Internet or an unsecured local area network.
91. **VLAN (Virtual Local Area Network)**: Any broadcast domain that is partitioned and isolated in a computer network at the data link layer (OSI layer 2).
92. **NAC (Network Access Control)**: A method of bolstering the security of a proprietary network by restricting the availability of network resources to endpoint devices that comply with a defined security policy.
93. **Port Scanning (The Scout's Probe)**: A method for determining which ports on a network are open.
94. **Nmap**: The most popular tool for port scanning.
95. **Wireshark**: A popular network protocol analyzer.
96. **TCP/IP (The Foundation of the Sea-Paths)**: The set of communications protocols used for the internet.
97. **IP Spoofing (The False Identity)**: The creation of Internet Protocol (IP) packets with a false source IP address, for the purpose of hiding the identity of the sender or impersonating another computer system.
98. **MAC Spoofing**: A technique for changing a factory-assigned Media Access Control (MAC) address of a network interface on a networked device.
99. **Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attack (The Hidden Listener)**: An attack where the attacker secretly relays and possibly alters the communication between two parties who believe they are directly communicating with each other.
100. **ARP Poisoning / Spoofing**: A technique by which an attacker sends (spoofed) Address Resolution Protocol (ARP) messages onto a local area network to link their MAC address with the IP address of a legitimate server or gateway on the network.
101. **DNS Poisoning / Spoofing**: A type of computer security hacking in which corrupt Domain Name System data is introduced into a DNS resolver's cache, causing the name server to return an incorrect IP address.
102. **DoS (Denial of Service) Attack (The Siege)**: A cyber-attack in which the perpetrator seeks to make a machine or network resource unavailable to its intended users by temporarily or indefinitely disrupting services of a host connected to the Internet.
103. **DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) Attack (The Great Siege)**: A DoS attack where the traffic comes from many different sources.
104. **SYN Flood**: A type of DDoS attack that exploits a part of the normal TCP three-way handshake to consume resources on the targeted server and render it unresponsive.
105. **UDP Flood**: A type of DDoS attack in which a large number of User Datagram Protocol (UDP) packets are sent to a targeted server with the aim of overwhelming that device's ability to process and respond.
106. **ICMP Flood (Ping Flood)**: A type of DDoS attack that aims to overwhelm a targeted device with ICMP Echo Request (ping) packets.
107. **HTTP Flood**: A type of DDoS attack that aims to overwhelm a targeted server with HTTP requests.
108. **Slowloris**: A type of DDoS attack that allows a single machine to take down another machine's web server with minimal bandwidth and side effects on unrelated services and ports.
109. **Amplification Attack**: A type of DDoS attack that uses public services (like DNS or NTP) to generate a large amount of traffic directed at a target.
110. **Reflection Attack**: A type of DDoS attack where the attacker sends a request to a third-party server with a spoofed source IP address (set to the target's IP address), causing the third-party server to send its response to the target.
111. **Botnet (The Shadow Army)**: A network of hijacked computers used to carry out DDoS attacks.
112. **Application Security (AppSec) (The Guarding of the Runes)**: The use of software, hardware, and procedural methods to protect applications from external threats.
113. **OWASP (Open Web Application Security Project)**: An online community that produces freely-available articles, methodologies, documentation, tools, and technologies in the field of web application security.
114. **OWASP Top 10 (The Ten Plagues of Code)**: A standard awareness document for developers and web application security.
115. **Injection Attack (The Poisoned Input)**: A type of attack where an attacker sends malicious data to an application in an attempt to change its behavior.
116. **SQL Injection (SQLi)**: A type of injection attack that makes it possible to execute malicious SQL statements.
117. **Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)**: A type of injection attack where malicious scripts are injected into otherwise benign and trusted websites.

119. **Reflected XSS**: Malicious script is 'reflected' off a web application to a victim's browser.
120. **DOM-based XSS**: Malicious script is executed by modifying the Document Object Model (DOM) environment in the victim's browser.
121. **Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)**: An attack that forces an authenticated user to execute unwanted actions on a web application in which they are currently authenticated.
122. **Broken Authentication**: Weaknesses in an application's authentication and session management processes.
123. **Sensitive Data Exposure**: When an application does not adequately protect sensitive information, such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.
124. **XML External Entity (XXE)**: An attack against an application that parses XML input.
125. **Broken Access Control**: When an application does not correctly enforce restrictions on what authenticated users are allowed to do.
126. **Security Misconfiguration**: When security settings are not defined, implemented, or maintained correctly.
127. **Insecure Deserialization**: When an application untrustingly deserializes data, which can lead to remote code execution.
128. **Using Components with Known Vulnerabilities (The Rotting Longship-Planks)**: Using libraries, frameworks, and other software components that have known security flaws.
